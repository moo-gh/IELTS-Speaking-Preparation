#### 1. What technology do people use most often in everyday life?
I'd say smartphones are **the obvious answer**, because they combine communication, maps, banking, and entertainment in one **handheld** device. Beyond that, a lot of people rely on laptops for work and study, and **household gadgets** like washing machines and microwaves quietly **save time** without us really thinking about it.

---
#### 2. Do you think modern technology has made life easier? Why?
Overall, I think it has **streamlined** many boring tasks—paying bills, booking tickets, and **keeping in touch** with family can be done in minutes. That said, technology can also create **new pressures**, like always being expected to reply instantly, so "easier" depends on how disciplined you are about **screen time**.

---
#### 3. What are some disadvantages of technology?
One major downside is **information overload**: it's easy to feel **bombarded** by notifications and **doomscrolling** on social media. There's also the risk of **cybercrime** and **data breaches**, and some people argue that heavy tech use can weaken **face-to-face** communication if you're not careful.

---
#### 4. Why do some older people struggle with new technology?
Often it's not about intelligence—it's about **unfamiliar interfaces**, small text, and menus that keep changing with **software updates**. Many older users didn't grow up with touchscreens, so they need **patient, step-by-step** teaching rather than being rushed. Confidence matters too; fear of "breaking something" can make people **hesitant** to experiment.

---
#### 5. How has technology changed the way children learn?
Children can now access **online lessons**, **interactive** quizzes, and educational videos, which can make learning more **engaging** if it's used well. On the flip side, screens can be **distracting**, and I'd say schools and parents need clear rules so tech supports studying instead of replacing **deep focus** and reading.

---
#### 6. Do you think people depend too much on technology?
In many cities, yes—people would feel **lost** without GPS, and some can't remember phone numbers because everything is **stored** in a device. I wouldn't call that entirely bad, but it's sensible to keep **basic skills** alive, like reading a map or doing simple maths, in case the tech **goes down** or the battery dies.
